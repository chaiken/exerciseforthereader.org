Current controversies

Recent research has led to considerable debate in the scientific and popular literature about which dietary fats are important. For many years, the issue of dietary fat was quite simple: reduce intake of saturated fat and increase the amount of polyunsaturated fat. This meant reducing obvious forms of visible fat (butter, cheese, meat fat) and replacing these with polyunsaturated oils (soft margarines and vegetable oils). But, it is no longer so simple! Questions have been raised such as, "are monounsaturated fats better than polyunsaturated fats?", "are the trans fatty acids in margarines harmful?", "are all saturated fats the same?" and "what about the omega 3 fats?"

The aim of this pamphlet is to sort out facts from fallacies in order to provide sensible, practical advice to their patients.

Not all saturated fatty acids have the same effect on plasma cholesterol levels.

The four most common saturated fatty acids in our food are palmitic, stearic, myristic and lauric acids, with palmitic being the most common. Palmitic and stearic acid are found in most foods containing fat. Many fast-foods are a rich in fat (table), sometimes referred to as 'hidden fats'. This fat can be a rich source of saturated fat since it is often made using palm oil (a source of palmitic acid). Myristic is most common in butter and tropical vegetable oils (coconut and palm kernel oil), and to a lesser extent in animal fats. Butter and these tropical vegetable oils also contain high levels of lauric acid. There is increasing evidence to suggest that the cholesterol-raising effect of saturated fatty acids is limited to lauric, myristic and palmitic acids. Since food fats do not contain single types of saturated fatty acids, the finding that stearic acid has a neutral effect on plasma cholesterol levels is of limited practical value. Remember also that while dietary cholesterol can increase plasma cholesterol levels, this is effect is small in comparison with the extent to which saturated fats increase plasma cholesterol levels. The National Health and Medical Research Council (NHMRC) (1992) has recommended that Australians reduce their fat intake, mainly by reducing the intake of saturated fats.

Practical advice for most people, but particularly those with raised plasma cholesterol levels (called hypercholesterolaemia), is to aim to reduce all major sources of saturated fat in their diet. Such advice could include eating less take-away foods (see Table), trimming fat from meat, eating skin-less chicken, using low-fat cheeses, milk and yoghurt, and avoiding excess use of butter and coconut.

Trans fatty acids.

Trans fatty acids are found in vegetables fats which have been hardened in the process of manufacturing margarines which can spread at refrigerator temperatures. These fatty acids also occur in relatively low amounts in butter, beef and lamb fat. Recently, high intakes of trans fatty acids have been shown to have an adverse affect on plasma lipoprotein cholesterol levels. Margarines in Australia do not contain high levels of these fatty acids by world standards (Mansour & Sinclair 1993) and the trans fatty acid intake in our diet is thought to be quite low (<5g per day) (Mansour & Sinclair 1993, Noakes & Nestel 1993).

From a practical point of view, trans fatty acids are not a major health concern to most Australian consumers .

Monounsaturated or polyunsaturated?

Monounsaturated fats have been considered to have a neutral effect on plasma cholesterol levels, however recent research has demonstrated a cholesterol-lowering effect of cis-monounsaturated fats particularly when these replace saturated fats in the diet. Studies in Australia support the effectiveness of low-saturated fat diets containing olive oil and olive oil based margarines in reducing cholesterol levels (Morgan et al 1993, Sanders et al 1994), as well as reports which indicate that diets containing cis-monounsaturates from other sources such as macadamia nuts, avocado and almonds are associated with improvements in plasma lipid profiles (Colquhoun et al 1993, Abbey et al 1994). The Food Industry in Australia has responded to this work by the production of a number of products rich in cis-monounsaturates in the last few years. Examples include cis-monounsaturated oils and margarines manufactured using olive oil, canola oil and Sunola, the latter being an oleic acid-rich oil derived from the sunflower plant by plant breeding techniques. The NHMRC (1992) recommends that the intake of saturated fatty acids in the Australian diet should be reduced and replaced partly by cis-monounsaturated fatty acids.

Practical advice for most people, but particularly those with raised cholesterol levels, is to aim to use monounsaturated oils or margarines (olive oil, canola oil, sunola oil) in place of butter or hard margarines, and foods such as avocados, almonds and macadamia nuts as a substitute for fast foods.

There are different types of polyunsaturated fatty acids

For many years, the only polyunsaturated fatty acid (PUFA) considered to be important in the diet was linoleic acid. This is the main PUFA found in vegetable oils, soft margarines and other food fats and oils. It belongs to the omega 6 family of fatty acids. In recent times, there has been increasing interest in the other family of PUFA, namely the omega 3 PUFA. Typical food sources of omega 3 PUFA include all species of fish (for the long chain version of the omega 3 PUFA) and certain vegetable oils such as canola oil, linseed oil, soyabean oil and walnut oil. The omega 3 PUFA do not appear to affect plasma cholesterol levels, however these fatty acids are likely to confer beneficial effects on cardiovascular health through other mechanisms (Kinsella et al 1990; Lorgeril et al 1994). There is evidence from Australian research that the beneficial effect of the omega 3 PUFA is most evident when the level of these PUFA is increased at the expense of the omega 6 PUFA (Mantzioris et al 1994). The likely explanation is that there is a competition between the omega 6 and omega 3 PUFA in the body. Prior to the recent introduction of canola oil, the Australian diet contained low levels of omega 3 PUFA (canola oil is not only a good source of omega 3 PUFA but it is also rich in monounsaturated fatty acids). The NMHRC recommends that the level of omega 3 PUFA should be increased in the diet (NHMRC 1992).

Practical advice for most people is to eat more fish (fresh or canned) and use more products containing omega 3 PUFA such as canola oil and walnuts. Because of the increased susceptibility of omega 3 PUFA to oxidation, the use of oils or margarines rich in these PUFA is not recommended for frying foods.

Antioxidants are important to prevent oxidation of fats in plasma lipoproteins

There is evidence that oxidation of one of the main cholesterol-carrying lipoproteins in the blood plays an important part in atherosclerosis (Steinberg et al 1989). This lipoprotein oxidation may be enhanced by polyunsaturated fatty acids which are prone to oxidation. There is no evidence that monounsaturated fats increase this oxidation process. Vitamin E plays a role in protecting polyunsaturated fatty acids from oxidation and there is suggestive evidence of a benefit from higher intakes of vitamin E (Gey et al 1991; Salonen et al 1991).

Good sources of vitamin E include wheat germ (and wheat germ oil), eggs, nuts and leafy vegetables.

Dispelling myths

Since all vegetable foods lack cholesterol, signs on avocados claiming them to be cholesterol-free are nonsense. While this fruit is quite rich in fat (table), most of it is monounsaturated. Nuts are also rich in fat however the fats found in nuts are mostly mono- or polyunsaturated. Australian research has shown that avocados, macadamia nuts, almonds and walnuts can be included in cholesterol-lowering diets low saturated fat diet (Colquhoun et al 1993, Abbey et al 1994).

What dietary advice should be given to people with hypercholesterolaemia?

The best advice is to reduce the amount of saturated fat in the diet. As far as possible, this fat should be replaced by complex carbohydrates and monounsaturated oils/margarines. The evidence from research around the world suggests that it is not necessary to attempt to reduce the level of fat below about 30% of dietary energy. Since the fat intake of many Australians may be more than 35% (NHMRC 1992), some reduction is in order. The Table below lists the total fat, saturated fat and cholesterol content of a number of different foods items (by serving). This is not a complete listing of all foods containing saturated fat, but it does illustrate that past advice which mainly focussed on reducing the intake of red meat and dairy products will not reduce the saturated fat content of diets which contain some of the foods listed in this table. In fact, recent research has shown that fat-trimmed lean beef can be included in plasma cholesterol-lowering diets provided the saturated fat content of the diet is kept low (O'Dea et al 1990, Morgan et al 1993). The NHMRC recommends that saturated fat should be no more than 10% energy in our diets (equal to about 20g per day for women and 30g per day for men). The table will serve as a guide to allow choices of different foods each day, thus contributing to a balanced diet.

Practical tips

In summary, preventative advice on dietary fat is as follows. Reduce the intake of total fat mainly by reduction in saturated fats, use monounsaturated fats where possible, increase the intake of omega 3 PUFA and food sources of vitamin E. Note there is some recent evidence which suggests that once people have persisted with reduced fat diets for a time, their preference to consume the normal (high fat) products is reduced.

In practical terms, this means that people should be aware of the hidden fat content of many convenience foods such as take-away foods, pastry, biscuits and cakes (often high in saturated fat). These foods might be a major source of saturated fat in a persons diet. Furthermore, where possible consumers should buy lean cuts of meat, trim any visible fat or skin from chicken, and use reduced fat dairy products. In terms of spreads and vegetable oils, use of monounsaturated products (canola, sunola and olive oil) should be encouraged. Finally, the omega 3 PUFA content of the diet should be increased by inclusion of one extra fish meal per week (either fresh fish or canned fish of any variety, particularly canned in brine) and the use of vegetable oils such as canola oil.

Low saturated fat diet for middle-aged male (example of one day menu)

Breakfast

  • Weetbix, rolled oats, bran, 2% milk, sugar
  • Cup tea/coffee plus 2% milk (two)
  • glass orange juice

Morning tea

  • Banana, sliced carrot
  • Cup tea/coffee 2% fat milk

Lunch

  • Wholemeal bread 4 slices, monounsaturated margarine, lettuce, tuna
  • Biscuit (oatmeal) or packet of dried mixed fruit+nuts
  • Cup tea/coffee 2% fat milk

Afternoon tea

  • Apple
  • Cup tea/coffee 2% fat milk

Dinner

  • Beef steak (lean), vegetables, bread (no margarine or butter), wine,
  • Stewed apple and low-fat yoghurt
  • Cup tea/coffee 2% fat milk

Snack

  • Glass milk 2% fat
  • Biscuit (oatmeal)

Notes : The use of low fat or reduced fat alternatives outlined in this diet gives a saturated fat content of 19g compared with 45g for the normal fat products. The low fat products include low-fat milk (instead of full cream milk), margarine in place of butter for lunch, no margarine on bread at dinner, lean beef (instead of untrimmed beef), low fat yoghurt (in place of plain yoghurt).

The saturated fat content of this one-day menu increases to 65g if the breakfast, morning tea and lunch are replaced by two donuts, a hamburger and 600ml flavoured milk.

Remember, the NHMRC recommends that saturated fat should be no more than 10% energy in our diets (equal to about 20g per day for women and 30g per day for men).

Questions to ask to help people reduce the saturated fat content of their diet

1. Do you buy any of your own food each day (eg. lunch). What type of food? If you buy sandwich or a roll, do you request less margarine or mayonaisse on the bread? Suggest consider making own lunch several days each week (it's a cheaper alternative). Suggest eat low saturated fat alternatives if eating at restaurant for lunch (try to include a salad).

2. Do you contribute to purchasing the food for the family? Are you aware of where the fat/saturated fat is in different food items? Do you purchase lean cuts of meat, low fat dairy products, monounsaturated margarines ?

3. Do you prepare any food for the family? What type of oil or fat do you use for frying foods? Do you trim the fat from the meat before you cook it? Suggest that grill meat rather than fry it. Suggest that after cooking place meat (chops, steak, sausages) on paper towel to soak up any excess fat.

4. What type of oil or spread do you use at home? Suggest use canola, olive or sunola oil products. Suggest reduce the amount of spread on bread when making sandwiches, use less or no margarine on boiled potatoes (try low fat cottage cheese), use reduced amounts of oil/margarine in baking (cakes, biscuits).

5. Do you use low fat dairy products? Suggest try low fat milk (2% fat), low fat cheeses and low fat yoghurt.

6. How often do you buy convenience or snack foods? What types do you buy? Suggest that keep a mental note of how often eat convenience foods and try to reduce intake over a period of time. For example, suggest carry fruit to eat when feeling hungry between meals.

7. Do you think that you eat a low fat diet? What do you base this opinion on? Suggest keep a list of foods consumed over a two week period and think of possible lower saturated fat alternatives (an analogy is that this "food accounting" is no different to keeping a cheque book or bank account in order). If the main sources of saturated fat are identified for a particular individual, it becomes possible to eat any food any time by making allowances for balancing a high saturated fat intake on one day with a reduced intake the next day, and so on. [Suggest purchase small book which lists fat and calorie content of foods (Calorie counter) available from Chemists for about $5].
