TUESDAY MAY 16, 2000
SCV Electron Devices Society
Subject: Flash Memory Technology Evolution - The Next Ten Years
Speaker: Greg Atwood (Intel Corporation)

The market for flash memory has been exploding over the past several years, driven by rapid growth in the cellular phone market and the emergence of new digital storage devices such as cameras and music players. On Tuesday, May 16, the Santa Clara Valley Electron Devices Society will host a talk on Flash Technology by Greg Atwood of Intel Corporation. His talk will address the challenges and directions for Flash Memory silicon technology to serve the rapid density growth and performance needs of these markets. Greg Atwood is a Fellow in Intel's Technology and Manufacturing group. He is responsible for the definition of silicon technologies for flash, networking, and wireless business. He was a member of the first Intel flash development program and has been a key contributor to the six generations of flash technology that Intel has put into production. Greg was responsible for the first Intel StrataFlash[tm], two bit per cell development program. He has been at Intel for 21 years since graduating from Purdue University with an MS in physics in 1979.

Flash market overview

There are two main markets for flash: code storage (cell phones, palmtops) and data storage (mp3 players, cameras). Right now cell phones dominate the flash market, but data storage applications are coming on strong. The flash market is growing at a 35% CAGR every year, and should reach 20 B$ by 2004. (Source: Semico.)

Cell phones

In 1999, cell phones accounted for 27% of the flash market, with digital cameras (the application most visible to consumers) accounting for only 2%. By 2004, e-books, music players, set-top boxes and GPS are all expected to be major consumers of flash.

Cell phone usage is growing at a CAGR of 30%. There will be 109 users by 2003. Computers and phones now fall on a continuous spectrum, with hybrid devices growing in importance. This convergence presents new opportunities for memory. With 3rd generation cell phones, expected in a few years, 2 Mb/s data rates are expected, meaning that transmission of video will be possible. Obviously this has data storage implications. In 2004, entry-level phones will have 32 Mb of storage, but "smart" phones will have as much as 0.5 Gb.

The US is behind in consumer adoption of cell phones, so we tend to be unaware of how far this revolution has already gone. Cell phones are commonly used as "electronic wallets" in Finland, where sodas can commonly be purchased from vending machines via cell phone using a technology jointly developed by Nokia and Visa. In Japan, sending friends digital still images is a common use of phones. [Visions of Cooltown.] A 35 gram phone the size of two side-by-side pencils is now available in Japan.

From the storage vendor point of view, the good news is that the incorporation of flash into phones is now limited by the cost of flash, not the phone manufacturer's need for memory.

MP3 Players

Music players are an example of flash usage for data storage. Market analysts anticipate a 100% CAGR for music player sales through 2003, reaching 8-16M units/year. In 2000, it is expected that 3.7M mp3 players will be sold. These unit volumes sound paltry, but "MP3 players are essentially just wrappers for flash. They have a very high silicon content." In 2000, players will typically have 64 MB of memory, accounting for 75% of the cost. Magnetic storage is not viable for MP3 players, which require rugged, low-power storage.

Digital cameras

For a typical digital camera, 10% of the purchase price is in flash. 8.8M units were sold in 2000. Magnetic storage is poised to recapture this market, since cameras themselves are neither rugged nor low-power.

Even though MP3 players and other data storage applications of flash have small unit sales, they should represent half the market by 2003.

The flash industry

The top 5 manufacturers of flash in 1999 (in order) were Intel, AMD, Fujitsu, Sharp and Atmel. The first four of these sold identical products. All 5 of these sold flash specialized for code storage (common-ground NOR architecture).

The 6th and 7th vendors of flash were Toshiba and Samsung. They sell flash specialized for data storage applications, and have been moving up the list. ST Microelectronics was eighth.

Flash Technologies

For code storage applications, read speed is all-important, since users will never load new code. For data storage applications, write speed is all-important. These customer needs determine the design of flash devices. Right now "common-ground NOR" devices for code storage represent 75% of total flash sales. cgNOR is optimized for write speed and can be extended to a multi-level cell (MLC). The most common architecture for data storage is NAND-based. NAND is denser than cgNOR and faster at writing, but it is not extensible to a MLC design. [I got the distinct impression that cgNOR is Intel's propietary standard and that a competitor has developed NAND, but I could be wrong about this.]

Right now cell sizes are scaling down at a CAGR of 32%/year. This means a 65%/year CAGR for product total capacity. Cell sizes have been halving every two years.

Problems with scaling ("Roadbumps")

  • Oxide thickness scaling limit

The gate oxide dielectrics in flash elements must be susceptible to tunneling when you want to write, but must be impermeable to leakage at other times. Contacts and other insulating elements in flash have scaled well during the 15-year history of the technology, but these non-transistor elements are now such a small part of the real estate that the transistor itself will have to be scaled soon. Once the transistor is scaled, the gate oxide thickness will have to be scaled too. (This is always true of CMOS.)

In 1985 when flash was introduced, the gate oxide thickness was 115 Å when the cell size was 1.5 µm. Now the oxide thickness is 95Å at 0.18 µm.

  • Channel hot carrier electron limit

Channel hot carrier electrons are a second way to program flash. However, the hot carrier method requires a 4-5 V power supply and the tunneling method requires 15-20 V. The high voltage circuitry at the edge of the die is difficult to integrate with the flash cells. [? It wasn't clear why this was so.]

In the past, flash designers have often solved problems by adopting solutions that CPU designers have developed. However, the two problems described above are unique to flash, so the CPU developers are not going to save flash designers this time.

Predictions

  • Multi-level cells (MLC) will become prevalent.

Within 5-6 years, 2 bits/cell (2b/c, which is already available) will give way to 4 bits/cell (4b/c). Going to MLCs is easier than continuing scaling.

In the future, some products will mix 2b/c (high-reliability) with 4b/c (cheap, high-density).

Flash is intrinsically an analog memory scheme, since the data is stored as charge on a gate. This is in contrast to SRAM, where the flip-flop can really only be in one of two stable states.

For two-bit cells, the state of the device is read out as the threshold voltage [presumably the threshold for tunnel current]. The threshold varies between 2.5 and 6 V for a two-bit cell, with these values representing 00 and 11. 01 and 10 are represented by intermediate discrete threshold values. Two-bit cells are only 5% larger in area than an array of 1-bit cells, meaning that 2b/c gives about a 1-scaling-generation increase in density without cell-size reduction.

2b/c has a 105 cycles endurance, a 6 µS write time, and a 120 nS read time. These specs are almost as good as typical cgNOR devices, so 2b/c comes with little performance cost.

ST Microelectronics has announced a 4b/c demo. At this density, performance is truly degraded and compromises must be made. [Is there an opportunity for quantum devices here? Certain solid-state systems, most obviously the quantum hall effect, give well-defined, stable threshold voltages. Not obvious how to make this into a memory, though!]

At the 4b/c level, a 1 Gb flash memory will be possible in the current form factor with 0.18 µm linewidths.

  • Flash will be increasingly integrated with logic chips, especially in cell phones.

[This argument struck me as Intel special pleading. They are famous for wanting to leverage their CPU leadership into domination of other IC markets, and this integration plan seems to fit in with their other world-domination schemes. Of course, that doesn't mean that the performance argument for integration isn't compelling.]

A cell phone = radio + signal processing + computer + memory. Which should be integrated first? Right now cell phones have 40 ICs, but Atwood predicts they will have 5 by 2004.

The best integration is flash + logic since that eliminates the need for a bus. Buses waste lots of power, so their elimination is desirable. This integration should be accomplished by 2003.

  • Magnetic storage continues to play a role in portables.

The demise of magnetic storage in the handheld market has been widely predicted, but Atwood expects that it will hang on in products like cameras, where ruggedness and low power consumption don't matter as much.

Questions from the floor

  • Q: What is the acceptable error rate for MP3 players? What error rate does 2b/c flash achieve?

A: The great thing about MP3 is that data archivability and error rate are relatively unimportant. In this application, users won't use up their 105 cycles, nor do they care about a few bit errors here and there. Furthermore, since users will want to rewrite their storage frequently (no listening to the same music over and over), years of storage reliability doesn't matter. However, at 4b/c, even MP3 players may need error correction codes, which they don't have now.

  • Q: What accounts for the shape of the number-of-devices vs. threshold voltage histogram plot?

[In the histogram plot, the 00 and 11 peaks for a 2b/c cell were very broad, showing a rather unpredicable threshold voltage, while the 10 and 01 peaks were narrow.]

A: Right now flash products automatically do error checking as part of the writing algorithm. The controller will write the bits to a location, then check the threshold voltage. If the voltage is not in range (that is, is not readily distinguishable from a state differing by 1 bit), then the controller will rewrite the bit until it is in range. The controller doesn't bother to do this for the 00 and 11 (2b/c) or 0000 and 1111 (4b/c) cells, since these endpoint values need only be above or below certain levels, not within a certain range.

  • Q: Might novel dielectrics solve the flash transistor scaling problem?

A: Dielectrics for flash must be good both for charge storage (like traditional dielectrics) and for tunneling. It's hard for a new dielectric material to compete with SiOx on both these accounts. There are good reasons why ­100 Å of SiOx has been used for 15 years.

Summary

  • Flash was a $4B industry in 1999.
  • The industry is mature, in that new cell designs are no longer appearing.
  • Scaling challenges are coming up soon.
  • Multi-level cells are the best way around oxide thickness problems.
  • Flash will soon be integrated with logic.
